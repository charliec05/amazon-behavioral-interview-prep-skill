# Amazon LP Behavioral Question Guide

---

## How to Use This Guide

This guide solves three common pain points in behavioral interview prep:
1. Not knowing which LP maps to which question
2. Not seeing that different questions can share the same answer
3. Not knowing how to actually answer (what IS a calculated risk? how do you go above & beyond?)

For each LP, the guide provides:
- **What's Being Tested** — the real evaluation criteria behind the question
- **How to Prepare** — how to excavate stories from your experience
- **Question Categories** — grouped so you can see which questions share a story
- **Story Flow** — step-by-step narrative skeleton (shown as A → B → C chains)
- **Story Checkpoints** — what the interviewer is actually listening for

---

## The "Packaging" Concept

BQ stories are NOT fabricated — they are **packaged**. All events are real, but you organize and reframe the logic/causality to match different LP evaluation criteria. The same real experience can be packaged into stories for multiple LPs.

**Example:** Boss assigned two features. For "how do you work above & beyond":
- "I noticed a problem that needed fixing" *(package as proactive discovery, not boss-assigned)*
- "I wrote a plan and told my boss I'd deliver in one week" *(shows planning, deadline = ownership + calculated risk)*
- "I shipped feature 1 — latency dropped 40%, CTR grew 15%" *(deliver results, quantify)*
- "I tested my own product as a customer" *(customer obsession — work backwards)*
- "I found it couldn't cover certain scenarios and proposed a follow-up feature" *(above & beyond)*

Everything is real. The packaging connects the dots with LP-aligned logic. Because everything is real, you won't fear follow-up questions.

---

## STAR vs STARL

- **Positive questions** → **STAR** (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
- **Negative questions** (failure, mistake, missed deadline) → **STARL** (STAR + **Learning**)

For STARL, the "Learning → later applied successfully" link is critical:
acknowledge mistake → quantify its impact → fix it → what you learned → how you applied that learning to later succeed

**Timing:** Aim for ~2 minutes. Hide some details so the interviewer can ask follow-ups:
*"I'd love to dive into the details later, but I was able to implement and ship it..."*

**Universal rules for every story:**
- Always say **"I"** not **"we"**
- Always **quantify** results with numbers (%, $, latency, users, CTR)
- **No villains** — never blame others or other teams
- For negative questions: frame the miss as standards being too high or solution not scalable — not your capability

## Story Reuse Map

Use this to reduce prep load. One core story can cover many LP questions.

| Core Story Type | Can Cover These LPs |
|---|---|
| Out-of-scope problem you proactively fixed | Ownership, Customer Obsession, Bias for Action |
| Pushed back on PM/boss, won with long-term approach | Ownership, Are Right A Lot, Deliver Results |
| Complex system you debugged end-to-end | Dive Deep, Are Right A Lot, Insist on Highest Standards |
| Failure → learned → later succeeded | Are Right A Lot, Earn Trust, Deliver Results, Insist on Highest Standards |
| Big ambiguous project you broke down and shipped | Think Big, Invent & Simplify, Deliver Results |
| Collaborated with partner team to simplify | Invent & Simplify, Ownership, Earn Trust |
| Helped a struggling peer or new hire | Ownership, Earn Trust |
| Interpersonal conflict you navigated | Are Right A Lot, Earn Trust, Have Backbone |

## LP 1: Customer Obsession

> Leaders **start with the customer and work backwards**. They work vigorously to earn and keep customer trust. Although leaders pay attention to competitors, they obsess over customers.

**What's Being Tested:**
- Can you think from the customer's perspective and solve problems that way?
- Note: "customer" = real end users OR downstream teams that consume your data/signal/pipeline

**How to Prepare:** Review your work — what problems have you solved for customers? Did you proactively discover and solve them? What was the customer feedback? How did you communicate with customers, and what was the outcome?

---

### Category 1: Customer Interaction (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me a time when you made a suggestion for clients.
2. When working with a large number of customers, it's tricky to deliver excellent service to them all. How do you go about prioritizing customers' needs?

**Question Analysis:** Both ask the same thing — understand what users truly want and prioritize work to satisfy their needs.

**Story Flow:**
> How you discovered the customer need → generated a plan → what you did during execution → what results were produced

---

### Category 2: Difficult Customer / Pushback (negative → STARL)

**Questions:**
1. Describe a difficult interaction you had with your client.
2. Tell me about a time that customers told you they want something but you know that's not really what they want.
3. When do you think it's OK to push back or say no to an unreasonable customer request?
4. Tell me about a project where you think the requirements are unreasonable or can't be done.
5. When your delivery is different from your customer's expectation.
6. Give me an example of a time when you did not meet a client's expectation. What happened, and how did you attempt to rectify the situation?
7. Who was your most challenging customer?

**Question Analysis:** All test the same thing — because you know what customers truly want, you push back or say no and provide a better solution. Communication is also being tested: how did you explain your reasoning and persuade the customer?

**Story Flow:**
> How you discovered the customer need → made a plan → executed → discovered some customer needs didn't make sense → why they didn't make sense → how you communicated this to the customer → customer's reaction → how you executed your plan anyway → customer's final feedback → final result → what you learned

---

### Category 3: Above & Beyond (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time when you went above and beyond for a client.
2. Tell me about a time you came up with a solution that customers didn't ask for and they ended up liking it.
3. Not only met a goal but considerably exceeded expectations.

**Question Analysis:** Tests whether you're unsatisfied with the current version and proactively continue improving product quality to further meet customer needs.

**Story Flow:**
> Discovered customer need → made a plan → executed → got results → in some situation you proactively discovered more problems (actively sought customer feedback? tested as a customer yourself?) → continued research → generated a new plan → executed → new results

## LP 2: Are Right, A Lot

> Leaders are right a lot. They have strong judgment and good instincts. They seek diverse perspectives and work to disconfirm their beliefs.

**What's Being Tested:** How do you make correct decisions when information is insufficient and a decision must be made? Was there deadline pressure? How do you collect key information from different angles and make a relatively wise call?

**How to Prepare:** Think about times you had to make decisions without enough information. How did you do it? How did you determine what information was essential vs. non-essential? How did you collect the necessary information?

---

### Category 1: Mistake / Failure (negative → STARL)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you failed / were wrong / made a mistake / had an error in judgment / made a bad decision / have regrets.
2. Tell me about a time when you made a bad decision and the learning from that experience enabled you to make a good decision later. What did you learn that you were able to apply?

**Question Analysis:** For failure questions: acknowledge mistake → quantify it → what you learned → how you used that learning to succeed later. The "what you learned" and "later success" sections are critical.

**Story Flow:**
> Introduce the project and its goal → discovered a problem after launch (customer-reported or self-discovered?) → what the problem was, how serious (quantify impact) → how you fixed it → what you learned → later encountered a similar situation and how you avoided the mistake through this experience

**Story Checkpoints:** "Are Right, A Lot" is demonstrated through learning from mistakes and developing good instincts. Nobody is right from the start — going through mistakes is how you grow and develop good judgment.

---

### Category 2: Interpersonal Conflict (prepare 2 stories — one where you were right, one where you were wrong)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague or boss. What is the process you used to work it out?
2. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague or boss, and **you were right**.
3. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague or boss, and **you were wrong**.

**Question Analysis:** If you were right → STAR. If you were wrong → STARL. Recommend preparing both.

**Story Flow:**
> Set the context (e.g., you're in a meeting discussing a feature) → conflict arose (you thought X, they thought Y) → explain why you thought X → also explain from their perspective why they thought Y → describe what solution you proposed to resolve it (e.g., run an experiment, do a proof of concept) → address their specific concern with a concrete mitigation plan → after proving who was right:
> - **If you were right**: emphasize their positive evaluation of you → quantify result and impact
> - **If you were wrong**: explain why you were wrong → what you learned → quantify result → how to avoid similar mistakes in future

**Story Checkpoints:** This LP is demonstrated through using communication and objective data to prove who is right. Communication is critical — show you deeply understood both sides' positions, the origin of the conflict, thought from the other person's perspective, and proposed a concrete plan to mitigate their concerns.

---

### Category 3: Judgment from Incomplete Data (positive or negative)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time when you had to work with incomplete data or information.
2. Tell me about a time when you had to work with incomplete data and you **failed**.

**Question Analysis:** Can test success or failure. Success → STAR, failure → STARL.

**Story Flow:**
> Introduce the project → you identified a potentially improvable feature but were uncertain whether to work on it → uncertain because many factors could influence the decision → what those factors were (budget, work priorities, critical path, potential user impact) → you couldn't collect information on every factor → you judged which factors were most important → why they were important → how you collected that information → how it helped you make the final decision → quantify result and impact → if failed, also mention learning

**Story Checkpoints:** The main test is whether you have strong judgment to know which information is essential to collect vs. non-essential — so you can make decisions under incomplete information.

## LP 3: Ownership

> Leaders are owners. They think long term and don't sacrifice long-term value for short-term results. They act on behalf of the entire company, **beyond just their own team**. **They never say "that's not my job."**

**What's Being Tested:**
- Your responsibility and attitude toward work — regardless of ownership, you willingly dive in, find problems, and attempt to solve them
- Whether you know how to trade off between short-term and long-term gain
- Usually tell a story outside your team/scope/responsibility — must emphasize why it had to be you, not the owner (e.g., it was urgent and the owner was on vacation)
- End with quantified impact

**How to Prepare:** Think about times you collaborated with other teams — were there situations where you stepped up to fix another team's bugs? Or discovered another team's feature causing user dissatisfaction, and you dove in, diagnosed, and resolved it?

---

### Category 1: Above & Beyond (positive → STAR; all these questions can share one story)

**Questions:**
1. Provide an example of when you personally demonstrated ownership.
2. Tell me about a time you went above and beyond.
3. Tell me about a time you decided to take on a project instead of being assigned it.
4. Tell me about a time you stepped outside your job scope and solved a problem.
5. Tell me about a time when you took on something significant outside your area of responsibility. Why was it important? What was the outcome?
6. Describe a project or idea (not necessarily your own) that was implemented primarily because of your efforts. What was your role? What was the outcome?

**Question Analysis:** All very similar — can use one story. Overlaps with Customer Obsession, so the interviewer may use one question to test multiple LPs.

**Story Flow:**
> A sibling team was working on a feature → you were actively engaged and gave feedback throughout → project launched → you tested it as a customer (shows Customer Obsession) → found a problem two days after launch → confirmed it through detailed testing → tried to reach the owner but they were on vacation → felt you had to fix it to protect users → formed a hypothesis (shows Are Right A Lot) → used divide & conquer to investigate deeply → found the root cause → didn't have permissions to change the config → found someone with permissions → sent an email explaining the problem → problem resolved → emailed the owner when they returned to report and propose improvements → quantify impact (CTR increase, users protected from impact)

**Story Checkpoints:**
- How you proactively discovered an out-of-scope problem
- How you diagnosed the root cause
- How you communicated with the owner and proposed improvements, their reaction
- The story shows you led the entire process — you had proactive understanding of other teams' work, not just your own scope

---

### Category 2: Pushback & Conflicting Priorities (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Did you have to push back on a request/ask?
2. If you have conflicting goals, how do you make trade-offs?
3. Tell me about a time you had to sacrifice short-term gains for long-term goals.
4. Tell me about a time when you had to leave a task unfinished.
5. Tell me about a time when you had to work on a project with unclear responsibilities.

**Question Analysis:** The core of all these: how did you sacrifice short-term gain to pursue long-term gain? You push back on requests because they only bring short-term gain. You leave tasks unfinished because the task only creates short-term value. For unclear responsibilities, analyze what's short-term vs. long-term and go for long-term.

**Story Flow A — Push back / conflicting goals:**
> You're implementing a feature → had a meeting with PM to discuss progress → PM thought it should be done one way → you thought differently → you explained your approach → you also understood the PM's approach from their perspective → you explained why yours is long-term gain and the PM's is short-term → addressed PM's concern (e.g., your approach might miss the deadline) with a concrete mitigation plan → in the end you still shipped on time → quantify result and impact

**Story Flow B — Trade-off / leave task unfinished:**
> You're implementing a feature → you made a plan, tech roadmap, and deadline → during execution, unexpected things happened (license issue, dependency problem) → the deadline was approaching → you proactively proposed two solutions:
> Solution 1: quick & dirty, can ship on time (short-term gain) but may have hidden risks
> Solution 2: systematic fix (long-term gain) but might miss deadline
> → took both plans to discuss with leadership → convinced them to go for Solution 2 → broke down tasks → found you could reuse existing components, greatly reducing dev time → re-prioritized your current work → still shipped before deadline → quantify results and impact

**Story Flow C — Unclear responsibilities:**
> Leadership mentioned in a meeting they wanted to build a feature but hadn't made a plan → you proactively approached leadership, expressed wanting to take the lead → through conversation clarified expectations and set a plan and deadline → unexpected issues arose during implementation → proposed two solutions (same as Flow B from here)

**Story Checkpoints:**
- When facing unexpected difficulties, can you propose multiple solutions, analyze short-term vs. long-term, use communication to persuade others toward the long-term path, and still ensure on-time delivery?
- Must specifically explain what the short-term gain is, what the long-term gain is, what challenges the long-term approach has, and how you overcame them

---

### Category 3: Project Transition (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Describe a time you transitioned a project to another person/team.

**Question Analysis:** Due to reorg or ramping up new people, you need to transfer your work to another team. As the project owner, you see it through and ensure a smooth handover.

**Story Flow:**
> Our team reorged → a large feature I owned was split to another team → I proactively wrote detailed documentation → proactively gave several presentations → fully supported them and answered all questions → their feedback on me → the impact of this handover on prod (can say no production impact, or that they later shipped features based on your original designs and you participated in guidance) → quantify cross-team impact

**Story Checkpoints:** What specific steps you took to ensure successful handover, what role you played after handover — your impact becomes cross-team impact.

---

### Category 4: Helping Peers (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Give an example of when you saw a peer struggling and decided to step in and help. What was the situation and what actions did you take? What was the outcome?

**Question Analysis:** Naturally fits experiences ramping up a new hire — new hires typically struggle, and observing their struggle during onboarding lets you proactively offer help.

**Story Flow:**
> A new hire joined our team → I gave them a detailed ramp-up plan and lots of documentation → they started off well → but I noticed they were spending a lot of time on one problem without progress → I proactively set up a 1-on-1 to unblock them → found out they were stuck on a specific technical issue (or going through personal difficulties) → I directly helped them resolve the issue → shared my relevant experience → they completed the ramp-up plan on time

**Story Checkpoints:** Show you proactively observed, proactively noticed the problem, proactively approached to understand what happened, then provided the necessary help to mitigate the problem and help them grow.

## LP 4: Bias for Action

> Speed matters in business. Many decisions and actions are **reversible** and do not need extensive study. We value **calculated risk** taking.

**What's Being Tested:**
- You need to act quickly while understanding potential risks, and fast-iterate to verify feasibility
- You know which risks are worth taking — which ones have higher ROI
- If consensus can't be reached, emphasize your solution is **reversible** and won't affect current production users (this is critical)

**How to Prepare:** Think about situations where you and peers couldn't reach consensus — how did you understand the risks of your solution, fast-iterate to verify, ensure your solution wouldn't affect prod, and ultimately ship?

---

### Category 1: Calculated Risk (positive → STAR for Q1–3; negative → STARL for Q4)

**Questions:**
1. Describe a time you took a big risk.
2. How do you take a calculated risk?
3. Tell me about a time when you took a calculated risk.
4. Tell me about a time when you took a risk in your work and then **failed**.

**Question Analysis:** "Calculated risk" means: you sensed the project's potential risks, evaluated them, knew the worst case, and whether it would affect current production users. If it would affect them, why still take the risk? You saw more opportunities than risks. Fast-iterate to validate your idea.

**Story Flow:**
> You led a project → boss gave vague requirements → you proactively clarified requirements and synced expectations → made and executed a plan → project launched → found the project had dependencies on certain libraries/data/formats causing imperfect customer experience → decided to remove these dependencies → but the challenge was significant → spent two evenings collecting data, making a plan → told your boss you wanted to further improve the feature → you saw more opportunities than risks → said you needed one week to deeply research each step → **the worst case: after 1 week, the plan doesn't work, but nothing will affect production** *(this is the core of calculated risk — worst case is only time spent researching, no production impact)* → explain why it was difficult → how you broke the challenge into smaller pieces → resolved each one → how long to ship → quantify results and impact

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Show you proactively find and challenge difficulties, bringing bigger business impact
- These challenges have uncertainty (risk) — you evaluated each, made a plan, and considered the worst case
- You saw the opportunities — since you saw the risk, why still take it? What made the opportunity worth the risk?
- If worst case occurred, what impact would it have on production? How would you revert?

---

### Category 2: Incomplete / Missing Information (can reuse "Are Right A Lot" story with different emphasis)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you needed to get information from someone who wasn't very responsive. What did you do?
2. Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information. How did you make it and what was the outcome?
3. Tell me about a strategic decision you had to make without clear data or benchmarks.
4. Tell me about a time when you didn't have enough data to make the right decision.
5. Tell me about a time when you had to analyze facts quickly, define key issues, and respond immediately to a situation. What was the outcome?
6. Tell me about a time when you worked against tight deadlines and didn't have time to consider all options before making a decision.

**Question Analysis:** Very similar to "Are Right, A Lot" incomplete data questions, but the emphasis differs:
- "Are Right, A Lot" → focuses on how you used incomplete info to **make the decision**
- "Bias for Action" → focuses on the **actions** you took before or after the decision (e.g., before: took action to collect essential data; after: had essential data, made the decision, then fast-iterated many versions)

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Can you identify and collect essential information? Many decisions don't need complete info — just essential info
- How did you fast-iterate? What options did you explore?
- What risks did you encounter, and how did you weigh and manage them?

---

### Category 3: Take Action & Move Fast

**Questions:**
1. Describe a time when you saw a problem and took the initiative to correct it rather than waiting for someone else.
2. Describe a situation where you made an important business decision without consulting your manager.
3. Have you ever done something without asking for permission from your manager?
4. Give an example of when you had to decide between moving forward or gathering more information. What information was necessary before acting?
5. Tell me about a time you had to compromise something to meet a deadline.
6. Tell me about a time you had to deliver an imperfect result/project.
7. Made a difficult decision with input from many different sources.

**Question Analysis:** Questions 1–4 are very similar to Ownership "above & beyond" — can reuse that story and template. Questions 5–7 are similar to Ownership "pushback & conflicting" — can reuse that template. Update the story to reflect how you fast-iterated (came up with different plans and tried them to pick the best solution).

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Can you propose multiple plans and fast-iterate?
- What's your understanding of project risks? How do you evaluate them?
- What key information do you focus on, and how do you collect and use it?

## LP 5: Think Big

> Thinking small is a self-fulfilling prophecy. Leaders create and communicate a bold direction that inspires results. They think differently and look around corners for ways to serve customers.

**What's Being Tested:**
- Can you create something new with big enough impact to drive business and serve customers?
- Think big, but **implement starting small** — begin with lower hanging fruit, use divide & conquer to realize the big goal

**How to Prepare:** Think about large, complex projects you've worked on. What big/audacious goals did they have? Were the initial requirements vague? How did you break down the vagueness, plan the project, divide & conquer? What were the lower hanging fruit first, and how did you progressively move toward your "big" goal?

---

### Category: Achievements (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time when you went way beyond the scope of the project and delivered.
2. Tell me about a time you took a calculated risk to achieve a professional goal. What were the tradeoffs? What was the outcome?
3. Tell me about your proudest professional achievement.
4. Give me an example of a radical approach to a problem you proposed. What was the problem and why did it require completely different thinking? Was your approach successful?
5. How do you drive adoption for your vision/ideas? How do you know how well your idea has been adopted by other teams or partners?
6. Tell me about a time you were working on an initiative and saw an opportunity to do something much bigger than the initial focus.
7. Tell me about a time you looked at a key process that was working well and questioned whether it was still the right one. What assumptions were you questioning? Did you end up making a change?

**Question Analysis:** Questions 1 & 2 overlap with other LPs. Pick your most challenging yet impactful project and walk through it from beginning to end.

**Story Flow:**
> Boss gave a vague goal (e.g., improve our product) → you initially felt it was vague, thought from multiple angles about how to improve and to what degree → found the boss, discussed your big idea and plan → clarified expectations and the project's potential outcome → decomposed the project into several components → identified lower hanging fruit → fast-iterated to complete the first version → first version launched → achieved results (quantify impact) → continued to tackle harder challenges — risky, but you weren't worried because the first version had already shipped → solved those challenges → completed second version → launched → results improved further (quantify)

**Story Checkpoints:**
- How you clarified vagueness and defined a vision that's genuinely big (impacting how many users? which metrics?)
- How you identified lower hanging fruit and shipped the first version
- How you took calculated risk on the next version
- Where the second version improved, what benefits it brought

## LP 6: Invent & Simplify

> Leaders expect and require innovation and invention from their teams and always find ways to simplify. They are externally aware, look for new ideas from everywhere, and are not limited by "not invented here."

**What's Being Tested:** This LP has two distinct parts — **Invent** + **Simplify**
- **Invent**: be innovative — you developed a tool that greatly simplified the debug process, participated in research that improved user experience, published a paper or filed a patent
- **Simplify**: use existing solutions rather than reinventing the wheel — if you need a system and a partner team already built it, reach out, collaborate, integrate it
- In both cases, your solution must be **scalable and long-term** — no short-term hacks

**How to Prepare:**
- **Invent**: Did you propose any innovative ideas to solve problems? Did you build tools that simplified the development process? Do you have research experience?
- **Simplify**: How did you collaborate with partner teams? What were your needs? Why couldn't you build it yourself? How did you know the partner team had an existing solution? How did you initialize and run the collaboration?

---

### Category 1: Innovation & Improvements (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. What improvements have you made at your current company?
2. Tell me a time when you solved a problem in an innovative way.
3. Tell me about a time when you invented something.
4. What is the most innovative project you've worked on?
5. What is your proudest/biggest innovation?

**Question Analysis:** All about invention — use STAR. Can reuse the "Think Big" proudest achievement story, updated to emphasize what you invented.

**Story Checkpoints:** Clearly articulate what you invented and what concrete benefits it brought.

---

### Category 2: Simplify (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about an out-of-the-box idea you had or a decision you made that had a big impact on your business.
2. Tell me about a time when you gave a simple solution to a complex problem.

**Question Analysis:** A simple solution doesn't mean compromising quality. When doing a project, if what you need happens to be what another team has already built, the best approach is collaboration — no need to reinvent the wheel, and it also creates cross-team impact.

**Story Flow:**
> You're building a large feature → one or several components would take very long and impact the ship date if done alone → you poked around and found another team doing similar things → reached out offline to a team member → found through discussion it was feasible → called an official meeting → clearly explained your needs → convinced the other team this is a win-win collaboration (you ship on time and impact many users; they expand cross-team impact) → only took one week to finish a complex component → project launched → quantify results and impact

**Story Checkpoints:**
- How you knew the partner team had what you needed
- How you explained your needs and convinced them to prioritize helping you
- What the final result was
- The simplification must bring long-term benefits (more stable service? faster ship time?)

---

## LP 7: Learn and Be Curious

> Leaders are never done learning and always seek to improve themselves. They are curious about new possibilities and act to explore them.

**What's Being Tested:** Do you continuously learn? The critical test is: how did you **apply** newly learned things **to your work** and generate significant impact? At work: learning tools, understanding other teams' work, reading docs. Outside work: papers, competitions, side projects, other companies' tech blogs, system design.

**How to Prepare:** Think about how you maintain learning, what specific things you've learned, how you applied that knowledge to work, and what impact it generated.

---

### Category: Learn New Things (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you did something outside your responsibility.
2. Tell me about a time when you influenced a change by only asking questions.
3. Tell me about a time when you solved a problem through just superior knowledge or observation.
4. Have you ever learned something new by yourself and ended up using what you learned to solve problems at work?
5. How do you find the time to acquire new knowledge?

**Question Analysis:**
- Q1: Tests you using learned knowledge to take action outside your job
- Q2–3: You learned something and proposed suggestions that influenced your team/project
- Q4–5: How you applied learning directly to your project

**Story Flow:**
> You love learning and willingly invest time in new things → you're very interested in a certain field → you read papers, blogs, company tech blogs → you learned that a certain problem can be solved with a specific solution → this inspired you about a problem at work → what that problem is, why it's challenging → you used what you learned to solve this challenge → the solution worked, you successfully improved the system → quantify metrics, results, and impact

**Story Checkpoints:** You cannot vaguely say you love learning. Must specifically articulate what you learned, how you used it to solve a specific problem, and what the effects were.

## LP 8: Dive Deep

> Leaders operate at all levels, stay connected to the details, audit frequently, and are skeptical when metrics and anecdote differ. No task is beneath them.

**What's Being Tested:** Can you methodically investigate complex systems? During deep dives, your assumptions should be educated guesses (not wild guesses) — you follow a general procedure, structurize all possibilities, then focus on impact.

**How to Prepare:** Think about experiences debugging complex systems. Any debug process involving 3+ components qualifies as relatively complex. What non-straightforward problems required control variable methods or educated guesses to find the direction, then deep diving to solve?

---

### Category 1: With Data (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Give me an example of when you used data to make a decision / solve a problem.
2. Give me an example of you diving deep into data to figure out the root cause of an issue.
3. Have you ever leveraged data to develop a strategy?

**Question Analysis:** Similar to "Are Right, A Lot" incomplete data, but "Dive Deep" focuses on how you effectively process data, extract insights from it, and use those insights to guide decisions. The process of extracting insight IS the deep dive.

**Story Flow:**
> Before shipping a product, needed offline and online testing → offline test showed everything as expected → online test found metrics differed significantly from offline → you started deep diving into both offline and online data and metrics → you had a hypothesis → used it as a guide: first looked at data X (no problem), then data Y (no problem), then data Z (found issues) → continued deep diving into this portion → found the problem → identified the root cause → why it caused the offline/online difference → resolved it → continued online testing → everything normal → shipped → quantify impact

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Show the process of discovering, researching, and solving the problem
- Must describe your initial hypothesis/educated guess, the multiple directions you tried, how you finally found the root cause, and what happened after fixing it

---

### Category 2: Big/Complex System Analysis (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a problem you had to solve that required in-depth thought and analysis. How did you know you were focusing on the right things?
2. Walk me through a big problem in your organization that you helped solve. How did you become aware of it? What info did you gather, what was missing, and how did you fill the gaps? Did you do a postmortem?
3. When you were trying to understand a complex problem on your team and you had to dig into the details.
4. Give me two examples of when you did more than what was required in any job experience.
5. Tell me about something that you learned recently in your role.

**Story Flow:**
> You worked on a project → describe the business needs (serve millions of users, needs to be scalable, responsive, high precision/recall) → because of these requirements, the project was challenging → project has N components → how they connect → completed first iteration → found a problem → your hypothesis was a specific component had an issue → used control variable method to isolate it → found sub-component issues → continued investigating → found root cause → fixed → found more problems → continued → finally everything smooth → shipped first version → launched second version → quantify results and impact

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Must describe why the system is complex (demanding business requirements)
- What specific difficulties you encountered, how you used your instinct to find the debug direction
- How you systematically investigated component by component, and the final results

## LP 9: Insist on the Highest Standards

> Leaders have relentlessly high standards — many people may think these standards are unreasonably high. Leaders are continually raising the bar and driving their teams to deliver high-quality products, services, and processes.

**What's Being Tested:** How you set and achieve challenging goals realistically. Break it down: what's your goal, how challenging, what's your plan, how many steps, are each step's milestones realistic?

**How to Prepare:** Think about big, complex projects you've tackled. What big/audacious goals did they have? Were the metrics realistic or stretch goals? How did you achieve them? How did you break the project into components?

---

### Category 1: Miss Expectations / Goals (negative → STARL)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time when you couldn't meet your expectations on a project.
2. What was a goal you didn't reach or wish you could have?
3. Give me an example of a goal you've had where you wish you had done better. What was the goal and how could you have improved on it?

**Question Analysis:** Negative questions — use STARL. Can reuse "Are Right, A Lot" mistake/failure story.

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Must explain why you missed — was the bar too high or the solution not scalable?
- Don't let the interviewer feel it was your ability that caused missing the goal
- Never blame others or other teams
- What did you learn?

---

### Category 2: Improve an Already Good System (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time when you've been unsatisfied with the status quo. What did you do to change it? Were you successful?
2. Tell me about a time you wouldn't compromise on achieving a great outcome when others felt something was already good enough.
3. Describe the most significant, continuous improvement project that you've led. What was the catalyst and how did you go about it?
4. Tell me about a time when you worked to improve the quality of a product/service that was already getting good customer feedback. Why did you think it needed more improvement?
5. Give an example where you refused to compromise your standards around quality/customer.

**Question Analysis:** Very similar to Ownership "above & beyond" — can reuse that story.

**Story Checkpoints:**
- The system is already good, but what specific room for improvement did you see?
- You improved it because you saw opportunities to better serve customers and increase business impact, and you could see a feasible solution
- During the process, demonstrate customer obsession, dive deep, take calculated risk as well

## LP 10: Earn Trust

> Leaders listen attentively, speak candidly, and treat others respectfully. They are vocally self-critical, even when doing so is awkward or embarrassing. Leaders benchmark themselves and their teams against the best.

**What's Being Tested:** Your interpersonal skills — how you behave and communicate with others. Communication is the key: must understand both sides' perspectives (empathy), know where the problem comes from, consider each side's pros and cons, and propose a plan to resolve conflict.

**How to Prepare:** Think about project discussions where there was conflict — priority conflicts, metrics conflicts, expectation conflicts. How did you handle these to win trust and resolve others' concerns?

---

### Category 1: Mistake / Failure (negative → STARL; can reuse "Are Right, A Lot" story)

**Questions:**
1. Have you ever worked on something hard and then failed?
2. Tell me about a time you made a mistake / failed.
3. Tell me about a time when you couldn't meet your expectations on a project.
4. Tell me about a time when you had to **tell someone a harsh truth**.
5. Tell me about a time you were 50%/75% into a project and found you made a mistake and had to change direction.

**Question Analysis:** "Tell someone a harsh truth" = e.g., telling your boss the project can't meet the deadline.

**Story Checkpoints:** Communication is the key test — how did you tell your colleagues/boss the project missed goal? What caused it? Did you have a backup plan? How much loss could the backup plan recover? Did the project eventually ship?

---

### Category 2: Interpersonal Interaction (multiple sub-types)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you uncovered a problem on your team.
2. Do you suggest something to the team to achieve a better outcome?
3. How do you handle teammates you don't like?
4. Tell me about a time when you had to work with a difficult coworker.
5. Tell me about a time you gave team members suggestive feedback.
6. Tell me about a time you had a conflict with your teammates — how did you persuade them?
7. Tell me about a time you helped your teammate.
8. What's the worst feedback your boss gave you?
9. Give me an example of a time when someone on your team challenged you to think differently about a problem.
10. Tell me about a time your teammates had a different opinion than you and you were persuaded.
11. Tell me about a time when someone (peer, teammate, supervisor) criticized you about a piece of work. How did you react? What was the outcome?

**Story Flow — Advice to whole team:**
> You found your previous production feature stopped working → deep dived and found another team's feature was the root cause → explained the specific reason → communicated with the other team and resolved it → realized this was because at deploy time you hadn't added gardrail tests → your team only had few gardrail tests → you rallied the whole group to advocate for updated gardrail tests per feature → so that when a new feature ships, the test verifies it doesn't regress previous features → everyone agreed it was necessary → everyone added them → this type of regression never happened again

**Story Flow — Receiving boss feedback:**
> Boss gave candid feedback about your weakness. You felt it was very reasonable. You committed to fixing it. Over time, boss noticed your significant improvement, and you developed good habits that successfully impacted your work efficiency.
> **Critical note:** Your weakness must be **coachable** (not "slow worker" type) — e.g., not paying attention to others' work and missing potential collaboration opportunities. The weakness must be the kind that can be fixed through effort.

**Story Flow — Receiving feedback from peers:**
> First understand why they gave feedback, their standpoint, from what angle they thought your project needed improvement → then you considered their perspective (you should conclude it's valid) → you were inspired, made a plan, implemented and shipped, saw noticeable improvement

**Story Checkpoints:**
- Can you discover team-level problems and persuade the whole team to adopt your suggestions?
- For conflict: emphasize you understood both sides' positions and had a plan to resolve the other side's concerns
- How you proactively interact with peers and help others

---

### Category 3: Earn Trust Questions (positive → STAR)

**Questions:**
1. Tell me about a time you had to earn trust quickly.
2. Tell me about an experience where you had to earn trust/gain buy-in from another group.
3. Do you collaborate well?
4. Tell me about a time when you earned the trust of your partners.

**Question Analysis:** You gain trust through communication and executing a plan. You understand the other side's position, provide a plan to mitigate their concerns, execute it, deliver good results — then they can trust you.

**Story Flow:** Can use the interpersonal conflict angle — reuse Ownership "pushback & conflicting" template.

**Story Checkpoints:** Communication is always the core test.

## LP 11: Deliver Results

> Leaders focus on the key inputs for their business and deliver them with the right quality and in a timely fashion. Despite setbacks, they rise to the occasion and never settle.

**What's Being Tested:** How you unblock yourself and deliver high-quality work when facing challenges and uncertainty.

**How to Prepare:** Think about projects from plan to delivery — what setbacks and challenges did you encounter, and how did you unblock yourself?

---

### Category 1: Deliver Results (positive → STAR; can reuse Ownership/Think Big stories)

**Questions:**
1. Describe a project that you not only finished but also accomplished more than required.
2. Describe a time when you had to face a particularly challenging situation while working on a project and what you did to overcome it.
3. Give me an example of a time you struggled with something and then turned it around.
4. Tell me about a project you finished where no clear instruction was given.
5. What's your most challenging problem/project?
6. What's the project you are most proud of?
7. What's the most complicated problem you've ever worked on?

**Story Checkpoints:** This LP focuses on how you unblock yourself — emphasize challenges in the project and how you specifically resolved each one.

---

### Category 2: Negative / Failure (negative → STARL; overlaps with Ownership, Think Big, Are Right A Lot)

**Questions:**
1. Describe a time when you didn't think you were going to meet a commitment you promised.
2. Tell me about a time when you had to leave a task unfinished.
3. Have you ever worked on something hard and then failed?

**Story Checkpoints:** How you unblock yourself, what you learned from the failure, how you applied that learning to your daily work afterward.

## Why [Company] — Motivation Questions

**When it appears:** Common in HR calls, occasionally the first question in a BQ round. Tests your motivation for interviewing.

**What's Being Tested:** Your understanding of the company/product, your career planning, what you can contribute, what you hope to gain.

**How to Prepare:** Browse the company homepage to understand the product, skim their tech blog, understand the business and challenges, think about how your past experience can help solve those challenges, and what you hope to gain from the role.

**Story Flow:**
- **B2C company:** Start as a consumer who loves the product → transition to curiosity about the tech behind it → realized it overlaps with your experience → sparked huge interest → understand the company's challenges → hope to contribute and solve specific problems → realize your career goals
- **B2B company:** Start from the product's perspective → explain what it does and its societal impact → transition to tech interest → same as above

| Common Question | Story Template |
|---|---|
| Why Amazon/Google/[Company]? Why make a move? | Love the product → researched the tech → found overlap with my experience → sparked curiosity → understand the challenges → hope to solve them and create more value → realize career growth |
| What are you looking for in your next role? | Love my current work and team → but feel it's time to explore → love your product → (same as above) |

## Final Summary

- **Positive questions** → STAR
- **Negative questions** → STARL: acknowledge mistake, quantify its impact, fix it, show how you linked the failure to later success
- **Always quantify** result and impact with numbers
- **Interpersonal questions** (conflict, push back): explain how you communicated, articulate both sides' positions, show empathy, propose a concrete plan to mitigate concerns
- **No villains** — never blame others or other teams
- **Always say "I"** not "we"
- **Package don't fabricate** — same real experience, different LP framing
- **2-minute target** — invite follow-ups by keeping some details back
